How it works
01

Polluted air enters

Urban air concentrated with nitrogen dioxide is drawn through the intake. The BMP180 logs ambient temperature and pressure to compensate sensor readings.

02

Sensors detect

The MQ-series gas sensor reads analog NO₂ concentration. The STM32 ADC samples it continuously, smoothing with a rolling average to filter noise.

03

STM32 decides

Threshold logic in the embedded firmware evaluates the NO₂ level. If it exceeds the safe limit, the UV control signal is asserted via a GPIO pin.

04

UV activates · TiO₂ reacts

365 nm UV light strikes the titanium dioxide coating. Electron-hole pairs form, generating hydroxyl radicals that oxidize NO₂ into harmless nitrates.

05

Cleaner air exits

The treated air exits with significantly reduced NO₂. A second sensor reading at the exhaust quantifies the reduction and feeds the dashboard in real time.

The chemistry
behind the reduction

When ultraviolet light at 365 nm strikes the TiO₂ surface, it excites electrons into the conduction band, leaving behind positively charged holes. These electron-hole pairs react with ambient water vapour and oxygen to produce hydroxyl radicals (•OH) — one of the most powerful oxidising agents in chemistry.

These radicals attack NO₂ molecules in the airstream, breaking the nitrogen-oxygen bonds and converting the pollutant into nitrate ions (NO₃⁻) that adsorb onto the coating surface. The net result: air with a measurably lower concentration of a compound that causes lung damage, smog, and ozone formation.

The STM32 microcontroller acts as the system's nervous system — reading sensor data via ADC and I2C, applying calibration, making threshold decisions, driving the UV relay, and streaming processed values to the OLED display and this dashboard simultaneously over UART.
